"Exploring Lohri: A Pan-Indian Celebration of Renewal & Joy"

The article provides a detailed overview of how the festival of Lohri is celebrated in different regions of India, highlighting the unique traditions and rituals associated with each. From community events in Punjab, special significance for newlyweds and newborns in Haryana, to the symbolic bonfires in Himachal Pradesh and diverse celebrations in other regions, the article encapsulates the spirit of renewal and celebration that Lohri brings across the country.

Region Celebration
Punjab
In Punjab, the celebration of Lohri is a community event. The day begins with children going from door to door singing folk songs in praise of Dulha Bhatti, a Punjabi version of Robin Hood who robbed the rich and helped the poor. These children are given sweets and savories, and occasionally, money. As the sun sets, huge bonfires are lit in the harvested fields and in the front yards of houses and people gather around the rising flames, circle around (parikrama) the bonfire and throw puffed rice, popcorn and other munchies into the fire, shouting "Aadar aye dilather jaye" (May honor come and poverty vanish!). Singing and dancing form an intrinsic part of the celebrations.
Haryana
In Haryana, Lohri holds special significance for the newlyweds and newborns. The new brides get gifts from their in-laws which is a tradition called 'Manana'. The brother of the new bride gives a gift to his sister in a ritual called 'Bhabhi Te Makhna'. The first Lohri of a newborn is also celebrated with great enthusiasm and it is made sure that the family participates in the celebration.
Himachal Pradesh
In Himachal Pradesh, Lohri is known as Maghi Saaji. The day starts with children collecting sweets and savories from the neighborhood. In the evening, people light a bonfire and sing and dance around it. The fire signifies the spark of life and according to the locals, it brings warmth and happiness to the new year. People throw puffed rice and popcorn into the fire, hoping for prosperity and wealth.
Other Regions
In other regions of India, Lohri is celebrated with different names and traditions. In Maharashtra, it is known as 'Bhogi', when people discard old and derelict things and concentrate on new things causing change or transformation. In Bengal, it is celebrated as 'Poush Parbon'. All over the country, Lohri holds a promise of renewal and spirit of celebration.
